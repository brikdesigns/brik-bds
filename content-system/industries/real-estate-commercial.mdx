# Real Estate — Commercial Brokerage

**Version:** {realEstateCommercial.version}  •  **Last reviewed:** {realEstateCommercial.lastReviewed}  •  **Review cadence:** {realEstateCommercial.reviewCadence}

Structured data lives in [`real-estate-commercial.ts`](?path=/docs/content-industries-real-estate-commercial--docs). This page is the narrative companion.

This pack covers **boutique commercial real-estate brokerages** serving three co-equal audience verticals:
- **Healthcare professionals** (dental, veterinary, optometry) — primary revenue driver
- **Commercial clients** (retail, food service, professional services, fitness, banks, car washes) — co-equal revenue driver
- **Land buyers** (Middle Tennessee residential, agricultural, hunting) — secondary driver

The `commercial` segment is the broader business-tenant audience — businesses looking for the right space, market, and lease terms to support how they operate and grow. It is **not** a capital-allocator / investor audience: MOB acquisition, 1031 exchanges, and cap-rate framing are explicitly excluded by this pack. Owner-occupant building purchases for any segment (e.g. a healthcare professional buying their practice building) are bundled into that segment's services rather than split into a separate investor pillar.

Graduated from `small-business` because this vertical requires a 3-column audience-pathway mega-menu — the structural navigation pattern the small-business pack explicitly flags as a promotion trigger.

## 1. Industry Overview

Boutique commercial real-estate brokerages differentiate on specialization and broker continuity — not scale. The principal broker is the advisor for every deal from introduction through closing, including build-out coordination and lease renewal. This is the structural moat against national firms that rotate junior associates onto smaller accounts.

The three-segment model means one brokerage serves clients who might otherwise need three separate relationships: a healthcare real-estate specialist, a land broker, and a commercial-tenant broker.

## 3. Audience Segments

### Healthcare Professionals

Tone: "Clinical precision meets warm guidance. More specific than other verticals — these readers are educated professionals who expect competence. Confident without being condescending. Acknowledge that real estate isn't their expertise and that's exactly why specialized representation matters."

Primary pain points:
- First practice location decision — high stakes, unfamiliar process
- Second location timing — when to expand, where to expand
- Buy vs. lease decision for long-term practice planning
- Build-out complexity — equipment, layout, timeline coordination
- Understanding lease terms that affect practice profitability
- Finding space that supports patient acquisition goals

Key vocabulary: operatory, build-out, tenant improvement allowance, TI, demographic analysis, patient flow, equipment requirements, lease negotiation, practice location, second location, medical office space

### Land Buyers (Middle Tennessee)

Tone: local-knowledge-forward, practical, place-specific. Buyers want to know the broker has walked the land, not just listed it.

Segments: residential homesites, agricultural/row-crop tracts, hunting and recreational (timber, creek bottom, wildlife corridors).

### Commercial Clients

Tone: operationally fluent, business-owner-respectful, focused on the levers that affect the business — foot traffic, co-tenancy, build-out, lease structure (CAM, exclusivity, escalation, percentage rent). These clients are running businesses; the broker's job is to find a space and lease terms that protect their margins and support growth.

Focus: retail, restaurants, fitness studios, professional service offices (attorneys, accountants), banks, car washes, and similar tenant categories. Owner-occupant building purchases (often via SBA 504) and multi-site expansion strategy are also in scope.

This is **not** an investor audience. The pack explicitly excludes capital-markets framing — MOB acquisition, 1031 exchanges, cap-rate-driven deal sourcing are out of scope. Where a commercial client buys their own building, framing is owner-occupant economics (SBA 504, build-out cost recovery, fixed occupancy cost), not investment returns.
